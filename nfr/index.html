NFR: Network-Friendly Recommendations

Network-friendly recommendations (NFR) is a new paradigm that aims to increase the efficiency of content delivery by the joint design of network and recommendation system mechanisms. NFR leverage the recommendation systems that are an integral part of popular content/video services (e.g., YouTube, Netflix, Spotify) to recommend to the user content that can be efficiently delivered (e.g., stored at an edge cache).

In our works we study fundamental, theoretical and practical aspects of NFR. Specifically, we (i) introduce the concept of NFR and define system setups where it can be applied, (ii) propose models that capture simultaneously the network, the recommender system, and the user characteristics, (iii) develop theoretical results and design optimal policies for content delivery and recommendations, (iv) characterize the performance of NFR approaches in real content services (YouTube) and with experiments with real users. requires new models joint design.

Overview

The NFR paradigm is based on the key observations: (i) content/video services dominate the traffic and are crucial for the user satisfaction, (ii) recommendation systems are an integral part of popular applications, e.g., YouTube, Netflix, Spotify, and heavily influence the data demand, (iii) network operators and content providers increasingly collaborate for better content delivery.

Jointly controlling recommendations and network resources, will enable to recommend contents that can be delivered in high QoS (e.g., locally cached contents; see Figure 1 for an example), and allocate network resources based on future recommendations/requests. This can lead to “win-win-win” situations for the content delivery systems (lower costs), the users (better experience), and content providers (higher revenues).

Figure 2 depicts an example of nudging recommendations in an implicit way towards network-friendly contents in a real system: a content that can be delivered in high-QoS and/or with low cost by the network can be added in the recommendation list (replace a low-QoS content) or be promoted by appearing in the top ranked recommendations (reorder).

Theoretical Contributions

We model the involved setups, formulate optimization problems and design algorithms for:

  • Recommendation-aware network policy design (i.e., design the caching policy, given the recommendation policy) [J1] [C1] [C2]
  • Network-aware recommendation policy design (i.e., design the recommendation policy, given the content delivery policy) [C3] [C4] [C5]
  • Joint network and recommendation policies design [C6]

Experimental Contributions

  • We design methods and systems and apply them in real content services (YouTube) to measure the performance of NFR in practice [J2] [C4] [C7];
  • and conduct experiments with real users to evaluate their perception of the NFR approach [J2] [C7] (see Figure 3)
